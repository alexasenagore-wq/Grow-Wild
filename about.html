My Gardening Journey

I'm a novice gardener dedicated to creating a sustainable, wildlife-friendly space that supports local biodiversity. I hope this website makes your walks a little more interesting and inspires you to experiment with creating your own garden habitat.

If you see this webpage and think to yourself, "Wow, I didn't know that type of bird existed in Northbrook!" or "Whoa, that is one beautiful butterfly landing on that unique flower!" then you've made my day.

How it started: Removing invasive species

Most of my yard was a monoculture of invasive species, like these daylillies. I removed them all by hand to make room for much more interesting and beneficial plants.

When I first moved to Northbrook and finally had the yard space to begin my gardening journey, most of my yard consisted of invasive plant species including:

  1. Daylilies
  2. Buckthorn
  3. Amur and Tartarian Honeysuckle (eliminated!)
  4. Creeping charlie
  5. Garlic Mustard
  6. Rose of Sharon (eliminated!)
  7. Callery Pear (eliminated!)
  8. Burning Bush (eliminated!)

The early verison of the yard had very little insect life, and I never saw fireflies or butterflies stopping by. Not only was the yard boring to pollinators, but it was boring to me. These invasive species looked messy and were a pain to continously fight back in order to plant more attractive flowers. My dream of bringing in freshly cut flowers from my pollinator garden into my house was put on hold while I cut back thorny branches or dug out a million daylily tubers.

Questions & Answers

I believe that homeowners have a responsibility to improve the habitat of our yards, for the benefit of all living things in nature, including our community. Also because the process is fun! It helps me connect with nature. I hope by sharing this page it inspires you to give it a try as well.

Studies have shown that fogging kills beneficial insects, but not mosquitos! Fogging companies market their services, but the science doesn't support their claims.

But don't lose hope...the best way to kill mosquitos is in their larval stage, using a mosquito bucket and mosquito dunks that you can get from Ace hardware for less than 5 bucks. It's cheap and effective. Not convinced? Let me know if you want a demo of my mosquito bucket or if I can help you set your own one up. For more info about this technique, check out https://homegrownnationalpark.org/mosquito-bucket-challenge/

But that is yet another reason I'm planting native plants.

Studies have shown that some non-native plants like Japanese Barberry can create micro-climates that support ticks. (https://www.cleannorth.org/2022/09/15/hate-ticks-get-rid-of-your-japanese-barberry/)

Additional good news is that studies have shown that yards with more biodiversity have less ticks. (https://davidsuzuki.org/living-green/rewild-your-yard-to-fight-ticks-and-support-wildlife/) Yards with more biodiversity attract tick predators (foxes, coyotes, skunks and hawks) and reduce the numbers of mice and deer visitors (animals which harbor the most ticks).

So my yard is actually less likely to support ticks than a yard without native plants.

No, I'm not. I cheer when I see a coyote in the neighborhood because they are hard at work keeping our Chicagoland rat populations in check. There are no coyotes living in my yard because the habitat layout does not meet their needs. I have a trail camera (see the animal sightings page) so I know that all the animals are just passing through my yard instead of seeking a permanent residence. If you are curious about your own yard, I recommend setting up a trail camera in your own backyard. You might be surprised at your own set of visitors!

We're so lucky that the Northbrook Garden club (https://northbrookgardenclub.org/) sells native plants at their annual plant sale in May. Also Gloria Dei Lutheran church has an annual Native Plant Fest event (https://www.gloriadeinorthbrook.org/upcoming-events/) that is open and free to the public.
